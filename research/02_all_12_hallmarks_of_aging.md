# The 12 Hallmarks of Aging — Full Map

Based on Lopez-Otin et al. "Hallmarks of Aging: An Expanding Universe" (Cell, 2023).

The hallmarks form a hierarchy:
- **Primary** (triggers) — Genomic Instability, Telomere Attrition, Epigenetic Alterations
- **Antagonistic** (responses that become damaging) — Loss of Proteostasis, Disabled Autophagy, Deregulated Nutrient Sensing, Mitochondrial Dysfunction, Cellular Senescence
- **Integrative** (downstream consequences) — Stem Cell Exhaustion, Altered Intercellular Communication, Chronic Inflammation, Dysbiosis

---

## PRIMARY HALLMARKS

### 1. Genomic Instability

**What it is:** Progressive accumulation of DNA damage — point mutations, chromosomal rearrangements, copy number variations. Both nuclear and mitochondrial DNA are affected.

**Key players:**
- Jan Vijg (Albert Einstein) — quantifying somatic mutations
- Vera Gorbunova (U. of Rochester) — DNA repair across species
- John Sedivy (Brown) — transposable element suppression with lamivudine

**Promising interventions:** NAD+ precursors (boost PARP-mediated repair), SIRT6 activators, CRISPR-based precision repair, reverse transcriptase inhibitors (suppress transposons)

**Obstacles:** Mutations are heterogeneous and cell-specific. Somatic mosaicism is essentially irreversible once established.

**Timeline:** 10-20 years to approved therapies

**Connections:** Drives senescence, contributes to cancer, interacts with epigenetic and mitochondrial dysfunction

---

### 2. Telomere Attrition

**What it is:** Telomeres (protective chromosome end-caps) shorten with each cell division. Critically short telomeres trigger senescence or cell death, limiting tissue regeneration.

**Key players:**
- Maria Blasco (CNIO, Madrid) — telomerase gene therapy in mice
- Telomere Therapeutics (Helen Blau, Stanford) — mRNA-based transient telomerase
- Sierra Sciences (Bill Andrews) — telomerase activator screening

**Promising interventions:** AAV-delivered TERT gene therapy, transient mRNA telomerase, small molecule activators. Blasco showed AAV9-TERT extends healthspan even in old mice.

**Obstacles:** Cancer risk — telomerase is active in ~90% of cancers. Balancing regeneration vs. oncogenesis is the central challenge.

**Timeline:** Clinical trials for specific diseases 5-8 years. Broad anti-aging: 15+ years

**Connections:** Directly causes senescence and stem cell exhaustion. Short telomeres trigger chromosome fusions (genomic instability).

---

### 3. Epigenetic Alterations

**What it is:** Age-related changes in DNA methylation, histone modifications, and chromatin remodeling. The epigenome drifts — some regions hypermethylate, others hypomethylate — leading to aberrant gene expression and loss of cell identity.

**Key players:**
- Altos Labs (~$3B) — Yamanaka, Horvath, Izpisua Belmonte, Wolf Reik
- David Sinclair (Harvard) — Information Theory of Aging, OSK reprogramming
- Turn Biotechnologies — mRNA-delivered reprogramming factors
- NewLimit ($105M, Brian Armstrong)
- Deng Hongkui (Peking University) — small molecule reprogramming cocktails

**Promising interventions:** Partial Yamanaka reprogramming (OSK), chemical reprogramming cocktails, HDAC inhibitors, sirtuin activators

**Obstacles:** Reprogramming overshoot = cancer. Dosing, delivery, and tissue specificity unsolved. Epigenetic clocks may not be causal.

**Timeline:** Tissue-specific trials 3-5 years. Systemic: 10-15 years

**Connections:** **Central hub** — affects expression of repair enzymes, telomerase regulation, nutrient sensing, inflammation, stem cell fate. Most interconnected hallmark.

## ANTAGONISTIC HALLMARKS

### 4. Loss of Proteostasis

**What it is:** Decline of protein quality control — chaperones, ubiquitin-proteasome system, autophagy — leading to accumulation of misfolded/aggregated proteins. Central to neurodegeneration (amyloid-beta, tau, alpha-synuclein).

**Key players:**
- Richard Morimoto (Northwestern) — proteostasis biology
- Arvinas, C4 Therapeutics — PROTAC targeted protein degradation
- Primmune — immunoproteasome targeting

**Promising interventions:** HSF1 activators, pharmacological chaperones, proteasome activators, PROTACs, rapamycin (autophagy boost)

**Obstacles:** The proteome is vast. Neurodegenerative protein aggregation has resisted decades of drug development.

**Timeline:** Disease-specific (neurodegeneration) in trials now. Broad anti-aging: 10-15 years

**Connections:** Tightly linked to autophagy, mitochondrial dysfunction (mito-UPR), senescence (SASP protein burden)

---

### 5. Disabled Macroautophagy

**What it is:** Decline of the cell's recycling system — macroautophagy — which normally degrades damaged organelles, aggregates, and pathogens. One of the most consistent features of aging across species.

**Key players:**
- Beth Levine (UT Southwestern, deceased 2020) — Beclin-1, autophagy-lifespan link
- Ana Maria Cuervo (Albert Einstein) — chaperone-mediated autophagy
- Guido Kroemer (Paris) — autophagy inducers

**Promising interventions:** Rapamycin/rapalogs (mTOR inhibition — most validated), spermidine (in clinical trials for cognitive decline), trehalose, TFEB activators, caloric restriction, intermittent fasting

**Obstacles:** Hard to measure autophagy in humans in vivo. Over-activation can cause muscle wasting and immune suppression.

**Timeline:** **Closest to clinical translation alongside nutrient sensing.** Spermidine Phase II/III. Rapamycin PEARL trial Phase II. 3-7 years.

**Connections:** Overlaps with proteostasis, nutrient sensing (mTOR), mitochondrial dysfunction (mitophagy), senescence clearance

---

### 6. Deregulated Nutrient Sensing

**What it is:** The four key pathways — insulin/IGF-1, mTOR, AMPK, sirtuins — shift toward growth/anabolism even when repair/catabolism would be appropriate.

**Key players:**
- TAME trial (Nir Barzilai, Albert Einstein) — metformin, **first FDA trial with aging as endpoint**
- PEARL trial (Jonathan An, U. Washington) — rapamycin
- Dog Aging Project (Matt Kaeberlein / Optispan) — rapamycin in dogs
- Metro Biotech (Sinclair-linked) — NAD+ precursors
- ChromaDex — NR/Niagen

**Promising interventions:** Rapamycin, metformin, acarbose, NAD+ precursors (NMN, NR), canagliflozin (SGLT2 inhibitor — extends mouse lifespan, ITP 2024), caloric restriction mimetics

**Obstacles:** Rapamycin is immunosuppressive at high doses. Metformin effect size may be modest. NAD+ human trial results mixed.

**Timeline:** **Most advanced hallmark therapeutically.** TAME results ~2027. Low-dose rapamycin off-label acceptance 2-4 years.

**Connections:** **Central regulatory node** — mTOR controls autophagy, AMPK regulates mitochondrial biogenesis, insulin/IGF-1 affects senescence and inflammation. Most interconnected after epigenetics.

---

### 7. Mitochondrial Dysfunction

**What it is:** Reduced oxidative phosphorylation, increased ROS, mtDNA mutations, impaired dynamics (fission/fusion), defective mitophagy.

**Key players:**
- Johan Auwerx (EPFL) — mitochondrial quality control, NAD+ biology
- Amazentis/Timeline — Urolithin A (mitophagy activator, Phase II)
- Stealth BioTherapeutics — elamipretide/SS-31 (Phase III, mixed results)

**Promising interventions:** Urolithin A, NAD+ precursors, SS-31/elamipretide, PGC-1alpha activators, mitochondria-targeted antioxidants (MitoQ, SkQ1), **exercise (most proven intervention)**

**Obstacles:** Tissue heterogeneity. mtDNA heteroplasmy hard to correct. Free radical theory largely failed — antioxidants don't extend lifespan.

**Timeline:** Urolithin A/NAD+ available as supplements now. Clinical validation 3-5 years. mtDNA gene therapy: 10-15 years

**Connections:** ROS drives genomic instability and epigenetic changes. Mitochondrial damage triggers senescence and inflammation (mtDNA activates cGAS-STING and NLRP3)

---

### 8. Cellular Senescence

**What it is:** Irreversible cell cycle arrest + pro-inflammatory SASP (IL-6, IL-8, MMPs). Senescent cells accumulate, resist death, and poison surrounding tissue.

**Key players:**
- James Kirkland (Mayo Clinic) — pioneer of senolytics
- Unity Biotechnology — UBX1325/foselutoclax (Phase II, ophthalmology)
- Rubedo Life Sciences — ML-driven senolytic targets
- Oisin Biotechnologies — lipid nanoparticle gene therapy for p16+ cells
- Amor Vegas lab (Cold Spring Harbor) — **CAR-T cells targeting senescent cells** (published 2024)

**Promising interventions:** D+Q (in trials), fisetin (Phase II), navitoclax (potent but thrombocytopenia), FOXO4-DRI peptide, cardiac glycosides, **senescent-cell CAR-T**, senostatics (rapamycin, ruxolitinib — suppress SASP without killing)

**Obstacles:** No universal senescence biomarker. Senescent cells have beneficial roles (wound healing, tumor suppression, embryonic development). UBX0101 Phase 2 failure was a blow.

**Timeline:** First senolytic approval for age-related disease 2028-2030. Broad anti-aging: 5-10 years

**Connections:** **Key amplifier** — SASP drives inflammation, exhausts stem cells, promotes dysbiosis, alters intercellular communication. Clearing senescent cells improves multiple hallmarks simultaneously.

## INTEGRATIVE HALLMARKS

### 9. Stem Cell Exhaustion

**What it is:** Decline in number, self-renewal, and differentiation potential of adult stem cells across all tissues.

**Key players:**
- Thomas Rando (UCLA) — muscle stem cell aging, heterochronic parabiosis
- Irina Conboy (UC Berkeley) — blood-borne rejuvenation factors, plasma dilution
- Tony Wyss-Coray (Stanford) — young plasma factors, plasma proteome aging
- Elevian — GDF11
- Alkahest/Grifols — plasma-derived therapeutics

**Promising interventions:** Partial reprogramming of aged stem cells, niche rejuvenation (senolytic clearance), NAD+ repletion, young plasma factors, neutral blood exchange (Conboy — simpler than young blood), ex vivo expansion + reinfusion

**Obstacles:** Immune rejection, tumorigenesis risk, tissue-specific complexity

**Timeline:** Disease-specific cell therapies in trials now. Systemic stem cell rejuvenation: 10-15 years

**Connections:** Downstream of most primary hallmarks. Senescent cells in the niche directly suppress stem cell function.

---

### 10. Altered Intercellular Communication

**What it is:** Age-related changes in endocrine, neuroendocrine, and paracrine signaling — including altered extracellular vesicles, ECM stiffening (AGEs), and deregulated Notch/Wnt signaling.

**Key players:**
- Tony Wyss-Coray (Stanford) — plasma proteome aging atlas
- Irina Conboy (UC Berkeley) — neutral blood exchange (plasma dilution)
- Samumed/Biosplice — Wnt pathway modulation (Phase III, osteoarthritis)

**Promising interventions:** Therapeutic plasma exchange (available now, being tested), AGE-breaker molecules (alagebrium — stalled), Wnt modulators, exosome-based therapies

**Obstacles:** Enormous complexity. AGE breakers repeatedly failed in trials. Systemic interventions have unpredictable effects.

**Timeline:** Plasma exchange available now (off-label). Specific modulators: 5-10 years

**Connections:** Integrates signals from senescence (SASP), inflammation, dysbiosis, nutrient sensing. The mechanism by which local damage becomes systemic aging.

---

### 11. Chronic Inflammation ("Inflammaging")

**What it is:** Low-grade, sterile, systemic inflammation — elevated IL-6, TNF-alpha, CRP — without infection. Coined by Claudio Franceschi (Bologna).

**Key players:**
- CANTOS trial (Novartis) — proved anti-IL-1beta (canakinumab) reduces cardiovascular events. Landmark proof.
- IFM Tre (Novartis), Inflazome (Roche) — NLRP3 inflammasome inhibitors
- Nimbus Therapeutics, IFM Therapeutics — cGAS-STING inhibitors
- Olatec — dapansutrile (NLRP3 inhibitor, Phase II)

**Promising interventions:** Low-dose rapamycin, NLRP3 inhibitors, cGAS-STING inhibitors, senolytics (remove SASP source), colchicine (**approved 2023 for CVD risk — first anti-inflammatory for CVD**), JAK inhibitors

**Obstacles:** Inflammation is essential for infection defense. Chronic immunosuppression = infection + cancer risk. Hard to distinguish pathological from beneficial inflammation.

**Timeline:** **Colchicine already approved for CVD.** NLRP3 inhibitors 3-5 years. Anti-aging inflammation therapy: 5-10 years

**Connections:** **The amplification loop of aging.** Driven by senescent cells, mitochondrial DAMPs, dysbiosis. In turn accelerates genomic instability, epigenetic drift, stem cell exhaustion.

---

### 12. Dysbiosis

**What it is:** Age-related gut microbiome alterations — reduced beneficial bacteria (Bifidobacteria, Faecalibacterium), increased pathobionts, reduced diversity, "leaky gut." Newest hallmark (added 2023).

**Key players:**
- Jeffrey Gordon (Washington University) — microbiome science founder
- Eran Segal & Eran Elinav (Weizmann) — personalized microbiome
- Pendulum Therapeutics — Akkermansia-based products
- Seres Therapeutics — SER-109/Vowst (approved 2023 for C. diff)

**Promising interventions:** FMT from young donors (rejuvenates brain/immune function in mice), next-gen probiotics (Akkermansia muciniphila), prebiotics, postbiotics (butyrate), microbiome-targeted diets. Metformin's effects may be partly microbiome-mediated.

**Obstacles:** Enormous inter-individual variation. FMT standardization and safety concerns. Causal vs. correlational still debated.

**Timeline:** Targeted interventions available now. Anti-aging microbiome therapy with strong evidence: 5-10 years

**Connections:** Drives inflammation via endotoxin translocation. Gut metabolites affect nutrient sensing (AMPK, mTOR), epigenetics (butyrate = HDAC inhibitor), intercellular communication. Gut-brain axis links to neurodegeneration.

### Gaps and Blind Spots
- **Genomic instability** — underfunded relative to its role as a primary trigger
- **Intercellular communication** — poorly understood, few interventions
- **Integration between hallmarks** — almost no research on combined approaches
- **Dysbiosis-aging causality** — still unproven in humans
